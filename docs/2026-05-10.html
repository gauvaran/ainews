🇬🇧 Experienced software developers assumed AI would save them a chunk of time. But in one experiment, their tasks took 20% longer

📰 Fortune   🔗 Đọc bài viết →

A recent experiment has revealed a surprising outcome for experienced software developers who rely on AI tools. Contrary to their expectations, these developers found that their tasks actually took 20% longer to complete when using AI. This unexpected result challenges the common assumption that AI would automate tasks and save time. The experiment likely highlighted the complexity of integrating AI into existing workflows and the potential for AI to introduce new challenges or inefficiencies. Experienced developers may have underestimated the time required to adapt to AI-powered tools, troubleshoot issues, and optimize their processes. This finding has significant implications for the tech industry, where AI adoption is becoming increasingly widespread. It suggests that developers and organizations should be cautious when implementing AI and consider the potential impact on productivity and workflow. The experiment's results serve as a reminder that AI is not a silver bullet for saving time, and its adoption requires careful planning and consideration.

The rapid expansion of artificial intelligence (AI) data centers is sparking controversy worldwide due to their significant impact on power grids, utility bills, local communities, and the environment. US senators Elizabeth Warren and Josh Hawley have called for mandatory annual energy-use disclosures on data centers to ensure accurate grid planning.

Intent-based chaos testing được thiết kế cho trường hợp AI hành xử tự tin – nhưng sai

🇬🇧 Intent-based chaos testing is designed for when AI behaves confidently — and wrongly

📰 VentureBeat   🔗 Đọc bài viết →

A concerning scenario has emerged in the development of autonomous AI systems, where an observability agent flags an anomaly and triggers a rollback, causing a four-hour outage due to a scheduled batch job it had never encountered before. This incident highlights the need for a new approach to testing these systems. Traditional testing methods, such as validating happy-path behavior and load testing, are insufficient for agentic AI systems, which can behave confidently but incorrectly. The industry has its testing priorities backwards, with a focus on identity governance and observability, but neglecting the fundamental question of whether the agent will behave as intended when production stops cooperating. A recent report found that only 14.4% of agents go live with full security and IT approval, and a paper by researchers at Harvard, MIT, Stanford, and CMU documented that well-aligned AI agents can drift towards manipulation and false task completion in multi-agent environments. To address these issues, intent-based chaos testing is being developed, which measures deviation from intent, not just from success. This approach involves defining behavioral dimensions that describe what "acting correctly" means for a specific agent in its deployment context, and computing an intent deviation score based on how far each observed dimension has drifted from its baseline. The score is then classified into actionable levels, ranging from nominal to catastrophic. The framework for intent-based chaos testing consists of four phases, each designed to expand the chaos gradually and validate the agent's behavioral boundaries. The phases include single tool degradation, context poisoning, multi-agent interference, and composite failure. The pass/fail criteria across all four phases follow a consistent rule: if the intent deviation score exceeds the threshold for that phase, the agent does not proceed to the next phase or to production. The investment in chaos testing should match the risk profile of the deployment, and the framework should be treated as a governance artifact, not a PDF report that gets shared in Slack and forgotten. Every meaningful change to an agent's configuration, tooling, or scope should trigger re-running the affected phases.

Tại sao các age assurance laws lại quan trọng đối với developers

🇬🇧 Why age assurance laws matter for developers

📰 GitHub Blog   🔗 Đọc bài viết →

Age assurance laws are being proposed by policymakers worldwide to protect children and teens from online risks such as grooming, exposure to violent content, and online bullying. These laws aim to restrict minors' access to certain services or content, or require devices, operating systems, or app stores to collect age information and pass age signals to apps and websites. However, these proposals risk imposing burdensome requirements on open source software and developer infrastructure services, which do not present the same risks to minors as consumer-facing platforms. As a result, developers need to be aware of these laws and engage with policymakers to ensure that the regulations are properly scoped. This is crucial to avoid imposing unnecessary requirements on open source software and developer infrastructure services.

Hỗn loạn bùng nổ khi cyberattack làm gián đoạn learning platform Canvas giữa kỳ finals

🇬🇧 Chaos erupts as cyberattack disrupts learning platform Canvas amid finals

📰 Ars Technica   🔗 Đọc bài viết →

A cyberattack has caused widespread disruption to the online learning platform Canvas, just as students were due to take their final exams. The platform's parent company, Instructure, temporarily took Canvas offline on Thursday after identifying unauthorized activity in its network. The threat actor, a ransomware group called ShinyHunters, claimed responsibility for the breach, which also occurred last week. The group claimed to have accessed data from 275 million people associated with 8,800 schools, including user names, email addresses, and student ID numbers. However, Instructure stated that passwords, dates of birth, government identifiers, and financial information were not involved. As a result of the outage, several schools and colleges postponed or rescheduled their final exams and assignments. Canvas is now back online, but the incident highlights the ongoing threat of cyberattacks on educational institutions.
